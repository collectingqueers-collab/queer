She/They

A muse on TV

By Polygraph

▼ View Poem

I think I lost my gender
somewhere between
the third repeat of The League of Gentlemen
and my mum shouting
that I’d rot my brain
watching “that rubbish.”
Fair.
Because once you’ve met Royston Vasey,
nothing fits back in the box properly.
I didn’t wake up one day different.
I just woke up
after it all
After the confidence of someone
who didn’t ask permission
to be strange
and never once apologised for it.
Cheers for that Barbara

On BBC Two at an hour meant for ghosts.
People who looked wrong on purpose.
People who didn’t tidy themselves up
to make anyone else comfortable.
And something in me went,
oh.
You can do that?
I think my gender slipped away
during The Mighty Boosh too —
somewhere between Old Gregg
and whatever Noel Fielding was wearing
that week.
When everyone was fluid,
and glittery,
and no one stopped the show
to ask but what are you really?
No panel.
No debate.
Just vibes and chaos
and a bass line.
People act like this stuff is new.
Like we weren’t all raised by television
that actively refused to explain itself.
You can cope with
a small town full of monsters,
blackface nightmares,
and characters who should legally come with warnings —
but someone being a bit ambiguous?
Too far.
Nation in crisis.
Sure.
I tried to find my gender again,
checked the usual places —
school toilets,
PE lessons,
forms with tick boxes that stared back at me
like I’d failed a multiple choice question
on purpose.
Gender used to feel important.
Very serious.
Very GCSE.
Like something you’d revise for
and still get wrong.
Nothing.
Turns out it’d moved
to the weird shelf.
Next to Inside No. 9,
where things don’t explain themselves
and the point only hits you
after it’s already over.
People ask when I “decided.”
As if it was a meeting.
As if I filled out a form
instead of slowly realising
I felt more at home
with characters everyone else called unsettling.
I didn’t lose my gender tragically.
I misplaced it.
Like something you lend someone
and never get back
but don’t really miss
Like a DVD without a case
If anyone does find my gender,
it’s probably in a charity shop
unlabelled,
slightly unsettling,
cult classic.
Like something you lend someone
and never get back
but don’t really miss.

What is real?

By Polygraph

▼ View Poem

Sometimes I wish I were a real girl
and I hate myself a little
for even thinking the word real.
Because I know the negatives.
I’ve memorised them.
Pain. Fear. Being talked over.
Being watched like you owe the world softness.
I recite them like a warning label
to stop myself wanting it.
It doesn’t work.
Some days I look in the mirror
and it’s fine.
I shrug.
I survive.
I exist without flinching.
Other days—
I can’t meet my own eyes.
The reflection feels like a draft version.
Like someone clicked “save as”
before it was finished.
Everyone says girlhood is pain anyway.
Periods and fear and walking home
with keys between your fingers.
Being told to smile.
Being told to shrink.
And I know that.
I know that.
I list the negatives like revision notes
to convince myself
I don’t want it that badly.
But knowing something hurts
doesn’t stop you wanting it.
Fuck Marc.
I don’t want some perfect version.
I don’t want soft-focus Instagram girlhood.
I want the stupid bits.
The embarrassing bits.
The standing in a bathroom at a party
fixing someone’s eyeliner while crying
for reasons that don’t make sense.
I want to look at my reflection
and not feel like I’m borrowing it.
Some days I almost see her.
Like she’s buffering.
Like the screen’s frozen
just before it loads properly.
And other days—
god—
other days I can’t stand my reflection.
I tilt my head.
I pull a face.
I tell myself it’s fine.
I tell myself I’m being dramatic.
And yeah—
sometimes I’m okay.
Sometimes I forget.
Sometimes I laugh and exist
and it doesn’t hurt at all.
And that almost makes it worse.
Because it proves it’s not constant.
It’s not dramatic enough to explain.
It just sneaks up on me
in shop windows,
bathroom mirrors,
photos I didn’t know were being taken.
I wish I were a real girl
in the quiet way you wish for things
you don’t say out loud
because saying them
makes them heavier.

made with love, thank you all - Polygraph & Ashton
