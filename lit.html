Gender

By Avery Stridgen

▼ View Poem

I’m a girl.
I said so when I was four
I found out ten years later
I. Am. A. Girl.

But I sometimes…

I’m a boy.
More specifically a twink,
And I’ll still wear makeup
And I’ll show off my skinny waist
And I’ll look at my flat chest and be fine because I’m…

I’m a girl.
I went to therapy for that very reason.
The dysphoria lead me down dark paths
And I can’t leave the house without my chest
And if someone things I’m a guy I’d cry
It’s pretty obvious I’m a girl…

I like guys.
That fact is indisputable,
I want one- a boyfriend
To hold me, his twink- no his girl- no…

I’m a girl…
I am whilst I’m writing this anyway.
It would be nice to explore, when I’m a boy
That’s not often but it’s often enough to make me… doubt.
And the pronouns feel right (most of the time) in a way “he” never did
In fact “he” makes my insides shrivel.

I’m a girl.
I know that to be true.
I also know that can change, and that's okay.
Girls the default- sure, but twink is still valid.
I hope I get to explore properly
One day.

Avery Stridgen

Best Friend

By Tub

▼ View Poem

Your soul blooms like a flower,
I feel the anger that resides deep within you,
The flower is desperate to grow thorns.

Your energetic yet calming presence,
Brings joy.
The memory of you brightens my day.
Seeing you bloom hugs my soul.

I’m slowing discovering this flower bloomed in brambles,
A feat very few could accomplish.
I can see why you hate thorns.

Your flower attracted me.

but
your thorns are just as lovely,

If only you’d realise.

Caption: This poem is about my best friend. He has endured more hardship than I know and yet he is still the kindest person ever. Whether it’s his dysphoria struggles, his past trauma, his family/financial stituation or something entirely different, he does not talk about it. I worry that he thinks people will hate certain parts of him but the more I get to know his “thorns” the more I love him. I hope he lets himself become the man he is meant to be - flowers and thorns.

Tub

Geiriau Newydd / New Words

By Aven

▼ View Poem

O goedwig ddryslud fy amheuaeth,
Dan ganopi cysgodol,
Disgynodd had fy hunaniaeth
A wreiddiodd addewid gobeithiol.

Nid hunaniaeth coll oedd hi, ond –
Wedi'i thagu gan chwyn aflonyddwch.
Dim lle i dyfu fel y dylai,
Na heulwen i oleuo’r tywyllwch.

Ond yn neffro’r wawr,
Treiddwyd y nôs lluchddu,
Ac o dân fy ninistr
Llosgwyd lle i’r had dyfu.

Lle bu tawelwch fy siom,
Clywaf adlais alaw cyfarwydd –
Tyfodd o’r had cuddiedig
Geiriau newydd i’r un hen gân.

Teimlaf enaid fy ngwir hun
Yn symffoni fy adnabyddiaeth –
Awel cysurus ar groen creithiog
Yw geiriau canfyddedig fy hunaniaeth.

From the tangled forest of my doubt,
Beneath a shadowy canopy,
The seed of my identity fell
And rooted a hopeful promise.

It was not a lost identity, but –
Choked by the weeds of unrest.
No place to grow as it should,
Nor sunshine to light the darkness.

But in the waking of the dawn,
The jet-black night was pierced,
And from the fire of my destruction
A place was burned for the seed to grow.

Where there was the silence of my disappointment,
I hear the echo of a familiar melody –
From the hidden seed grew
New words to the same old song.

I feel the soul of my true self
In the symphony of my recognition –
A comforting breeze on scarred skin
Are the perceived words of my identity.

Aven

Eighteen Never Came

By Ashton

▼ View Poem

We were fifteen and lived behind screens,
Planning a wedding for when we turned eighteen.
You were my first love and my whole world,
But then you broke every promise you made.

You cheated twice and you hurt my body,
Taking things that weren't yours to take.
I had to leave to save myself,
But a small part of me still wonders.

I wonder if I’d be happy or just hollow,
If I had stayed and let the years go by.
I know it would have ended badly,
But I still miss the boy I thought you were.

Geiriau Coll / Lost Words

By Aven

▼ View Poem

Anifyr yw'r anwybod
Sut i fy nisgrifo.
Anifyr yw'r diffyg adnabod
Sy'n fy nghaethiwo.

Unigrwydd, heb dealltwriaeth
Gan fy hunain nag eraill.
Beth yw bod yn unigryw?
Teimlo'n unig, heb cyfaill.

Yn waeth na'r ddryswch,
Yw'ch geiriau cas am ein cymdeithas.
A'ch geiriau gwag o gefnogaeth: Celwyddion tryoloyw, dibwrpas.

Yn tawelwch fy siom
Clywaf adlais y geiriau coll
Geiriau derbynniol a chefnogol
Ochenaid breuddwydiol ar y gwynt.

Yn tawelwch fy syndod
Teimlaf enaid y geiriau coll
Geiriau all fyth cydnabod
Fy hunaniaeth difancoll.

Unsettling is the unknowing
How to describe myself.
Unsettling is the lack of recognition
That enslaves me.

Loneliness, without understanding
From myself or others.
What is being unique?
Feeling alone, without companionship.

Worse than the confusion,
Are your nasty words about our society. And your empty words of support: Transparent,
pointless lies.

In the silence of my disappointment
,I hear the echo of the lost words. Accepting and supportive words:
A dreamed sigh on the wind.

In the silence my surprise
I feel the soul of the lost words
Words that can never recognize
My lost identity

Aven

queer

By Tub

▼ View Poem

This doesn’t feel queer,
This doesn’t feel gay,
Well, this doesn’t feel straight either.

It feels like us. 

Through a life of feeling unnatural
A life I didn’t feel present in
A life where everything “normal” I did felt queer

This is the most normal I’ve ever been;
The most natural thing I’ve ever felt was us.

So, no
This is not special.
In fact it’s the one of only unspecial thing I’ve ever experienced

Which I guess does makes it special in a queer, gay, straight kind of way.

Caption: A poem about the only person I’ve ever had a crush on (and who’s now my partner <3). I use the word “queer” with all its meanings. This poem is an expression of how people find my sexuality/relationship the weirdest thing about me (and are desperate to label it) but to me it’s the one of the most normal and natural things I’ve ever experienced.

A muse on TV

By Polygraph

▼ View Poem

I think I lost my gender
somewhere between
the third repeat of The League of Gentlemen
and my mum shouting
that I’d rot my brain
watching “that rubbish.”
Fair.
Because once you’ve met Royston Vasey,
nothing fits back in the box properly.
I didn’t wake up one day different.
I just woke up
after it all
After the confidence of someone
who didn’t ask permission
to be strange
and never once apologised for it.
Cheers for that Barbara

On BBC Two at an hour meant for ghosts.
People who looked wrong on purpose.
People who didn’t tidy themselves up
to make anyone else comfortable.
And something in me went,
oh.
You can do that?
I think my gender slipped away
during The Mighty Boosh too —
somewhere between Old Gregg
and whatever Noel Fielding was wearing
that week.
When everyone was fluid,
and glittery,
and no one stopped the show
to ask but what are you really?
No panel.
No debate.
Just vibes and chaos
and a bass line.
People act like this stuff is new.
Like we weren’t all raised by television
that actively refused to explain itself.
You can cope with
a small town full of monsters,
blackface nightmares,
and characters who should legally come with warnings —
but someone being a bit ambiguous?
Too far.
Nation in crisis.
Sure.
I tried to find my gender again,
checked the usual places —
school toilets,
PE lessons,
forms with tick boxes that stared back at me
like I’d failed a multiple choice question
on purpose.
Gender used to feel important.
Very serious.
Very GCSE.
Like something you’d revise for
and still get wrong.
Nothing.
Turns out it’d moved
to the weird shelf.
Next to Inside No. 9,
where things don’t explain themselves
and the point only hits you
after it’s already over.
People ask when I “decided.”
As if it was a meeting.
As if I filled out a form
instead of slowly realising
I felt more at home
with characters everyone else called unsettling.
I didn’t lose my gender tragically.
I misplaced it.
Like something you lend someone
and never get back
but don’t really miss
Like a DVD without a case
If anyone does find my gender,
it’s probably in a charity shop
unlabelled,
slightly unsettling,
cult classic.

What is real?

By Polygraph

▼ View Poem

Sometimes I wish I were a real girl
and I hate myself a little
for even thinking the word real.
Because I know the negatives.
I’ve memorised them.
Pain. Fear. Being talked over.
Being watched like you owe the world softness.
I recite them like a warning label
to stop myself wanting it.
It doesn’t work.
Some days I look in the mirror
and it’s fine.
I shrug.
I survive.
I exist without flinching.
Other days—
I can’t meet my own eyes.
The reflection feels like a draft version.
Like someone clicked “save as”
before it was finished.
Everyone says girlhood is pain anyway.
Periods and fear and walking home
with keys between your fingers.
Being told to smile.
Being told to shrink.
And I know that.
I know that.
I list the negatives like revision notes
to convince myself
I don’t want it that badly.
But knowing something hurts
doesn’t stop you wanting it.
Fuck Marc.
I don’t want some perfect version.
I don’t want soft-focus Instagram girlhood.
I want the stupid bits.
The embarrassing bits.
The standing in a bathroom at a party
fixing someone’s eyeliner while crying
for reasons that don’t make sense.
I want to look at my reflection
and not feel like I’m borrowing it.
Some days I almost see her.
Like she’s buffering.
Like the screen’s frozen
just before it loads properly.
And other days—
god—
other days I can’t stand my reflection.
I tilt my head.
I pull a face.
I tell myself it’s fine.
I tell myself I’m being dramatic.
And yeah—
sometimes I’m okay.
Sometimes I forget.
Sometimes I laugh and exist
and it doesn’t hurt at all.
And that almost makes it worse.
Because it proves it’s not constant.
It’s not dramatic enough to explain.
It just sneaks up on me
in shop windows,
bathroom mirrors,
photos I didn’t know were being taken.
I wish I were a real girl
in the quiet way you wish for things
you don’t say out loud
because saying them
makes them heavier.

Polygraph

Love

By Sere

▼ View Poem

I like girls.
I think I've always known that, even if I never realised.
I like their smiles, their laughs, their tough softness.
I like them more than anything.

I also like boys.
Or maybe that's just what society says.
I think I may never know.
But I think I like them.

I like enbies.
I like how they're not afraid to be themselves
Or even when they are, how they do it anyway.
I will always like them.

I love people.
Anyone and everyone who lives or has lived.
I love their life, their light, their very being.
I love how they can always find a light in the dark.

I love love.
How it makes everything feel so warm and fuzzy and nice.
Platonic, romantic, familial, I love it all.
Love is a beautiful thing, regardless of the enactors.

The only thing I truly hate is society.
How it twists everything beautiful into something hard and painful.
How it worms its way into our minds and makes everything dark and black.
How when people try to be happy, it squashes their joy.

I hope one day society will be different.
Already there are people lighting up its gloom and cutting through its smog.
I love that hope.
It may not be enough yet, but it will be one day.
And I will love that day with all my heart.

Sere
